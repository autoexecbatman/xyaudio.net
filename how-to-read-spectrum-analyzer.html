How to Read A Spectrum Analyzer

Master the essential tool for mixing and mastering. Learn professional techniques for reading frequency spectrums, identifying resonances, fixing frequency masking, and achieving balanced, commercial-quality mixes.

Understanding the Spectrum Display

Horizontal Axis - Frequency

The horizontal (X) axis represents frequency, typically from 20 Hz to 20 kHz - the range of human hearing. Lower frequencies (bass) appear on the left, higher frequencies (treble) on the right. Most analyzers offer both linear and logarithmic scales.

‚ÜïÔ∏è

Vertical Axis - Amplitude

The vertical (Y) axis shows amplitude or power of each frequency component, measured in decibels (dB). Taller peaks indicate louder frequencies. Professional mixing typically aims for balanced amplitude across the spectrum without extreme peaks or valleys.

Real-Time vs Peak Hold

Real-time mode shows instantaneous frequency content - constantly changing with your audio. Peak hold mode captures and displays the maximum amplitude reached at each frequency over time, making it easier to spot resonances and compare levels.

Audio Frequency Ranges Explained

Understanding frequency ranges is crucial for mixing. Each band has specific characteristics and contains different instruments. Here's the professional breakdown used by audio engineers worldwide.

Sub-Bass: 20-60 Hz

  • Feel vs Hear: More felt than heard
  • Instruments: Synthesizers, bass guitar (lowest notes extend to 40 Hz), kick drum fundamentals
  • Mixing Tip: Keep this range clean - too much energy here causes muddiness
  • Common Issue: Rumble and room noise live here
  • Action: High-pass filter unnecessary content below instrument fundamentals

Bass: 60-250 Hz

  • The Foundation: Where most bass energy lives (90-200 Hz)
  • Instruments: Bass guitar, kick drum, toms, low piano, male vocals (fundamentals)
  • Warmth Zone: 250 Hz adds warmth without losing definition
  • Common Issue: Too much at 120-150 Hz causes boominess
  • Mixing Tip: Ensure bass and kick don't fight - give each their own space

Low-Midrange: 250-500 Hz

  • Body & Warmth: Adds fullness to instruments
  • Instruments: Guitars, keyboards, snare drums, vocals
  • Warning: Too much around 500 Hz makes mixes sound "boxy"
  • Common Issue: Frequency masking problems start here
  • Action: Notch problematic resonances, give each instrument space

Midrange: 500-2000 Hz

  • Vocal Territory: Critical for vocal presence and intelligibility
  • Instruments: Vocals, guitars, piano, brass, strings
  • Character: 1000 Hz boost gives "horn-like" quality
  • Mixing Tip: This is where your mix lives - balance carefully
  • Common Issue: Harsh, nasal tones if overemphasized

Upper-Midrange: 2-4 kHz

  • Attack & Definition: Where transients and consonants live
  • Instruments: Vocal consonants, snare crack, guitar pick attack
  • Clarity: Cutting through a dense mix happens here
  • Warning: Too much causes listener fatigue
  • Critical Frequency: 2.5-3 kHz is most sensitive for human hearing

Presence: 4-6 kHz

  • Clarity & Detail: Makes elements sound "present" and close
  • Instruments: Cymbals, hi-hats, vocal sibilance, string harmonics
  • Mix Position: Boost for upfront sound, cut to push back
  • Common Issue: Harshness and sibilance (excessive "S" sounds)
  • Tool: De-essers target 5-8 kHz for vocals

Brilliance: 6-20 kHz

  • Air & Sparkle: High-frequency extension and detail
  • Instruments: Cymbals, hi-hats, acoustic guitar shimmer, vocal breathiness
  • Professional Sound: Smooth rolloff prevents digital harshness
  • Mixing Tip: Shelving boost at 10 kHz adds "air" to mix
  • Warning: Too much causes brittle, digital sound

Professional Spectrum Analysis Techniques

1. Identifying Resonances

What to Look For: Sharp, narrow peaks in the upper-midrange (2-8 kHz) or broader bumps in low-mids and bass (100-500 Hz).

Why It Matters: Resonances create harsh, unpleasant tones that stick out in your mix.

How to Fix: Use a narrow Q EQ cut at the resonant frequency. Solo the track, sweep an EQ boost to find the harsh spot, then cut it by 2-6 dB.

Pro Tip: Don't rely only on the analyzer - your ears are the final judge.

2. Reference Track Comparison

The Technique: Compare your mix's spectrum to a commercial reference track in the same genre by routing both through your analyzer.

Critical Rule: Never compare static snapshots. Watch how the spectrums behave in real-time during similar sections.

What to Compare: Overall spectral balance, bass-to-treble ratio, midrange density, peak levels in each frequency range.

Warning: Every song is different. Don't try to copy the reference exactly - use it as a guide for general balance.

3. Finding Unwanted Frequencies

Low-End Cleanup: Play the track's lowest note/chord. Look for the lowest frequency bump - that's the fundamental. Safely cut everything below it.

Why This Works: Removes rumble, room noise, and mic handling without affecting the actual sound.

Example: If bass guitar's fundamental is 80 Hz, high-pass filter at 40-60 Hz to remove subsonics.

Pro Tip: Don't be aggressive - gentle slopes (12-18 dB/octave) sound more natural than brick-wall filters.

4. Fixing Frequency Masking

The Problem: When two sounds occupy the same frequency range, they compete and the louder one "masks" the quieter one.

Visual Detection: If bass and kick both peak at 100 Hz, they're fighting. If guitar and vocals both dominate 1-2 kHz, they'll mask each other.

The Solution: Give each element its own sonic space. Cut 80-120 Hz from bass where kick lives. Notch guitars at vocal fundamental (200-400 Hz).

Golden Rule: Each sound should have a clear, definable space in both frequency and stereo field.

5. Checking Overall Balance

The Pink Noise Method: Pink noise has equal energy per octave. A well-balanced mix approximates this curve when viewed on a spectrum analyzer.

What Balanced Looks Like: Gentle downward slope from bass to treble. No extreme peaks or valleys. Energy distributed across the spectrum.

Common Problems: Too much energy in 200-400 Hz (muddy), excessive 2-4 kHz (harsh), weak sub-bass or air frequencies.

Remember: This is a guide, not a rule. Genre matters - EDM has more bass energy, acoustic jazz has more mids.

6. Monitoring While Mixing

Real-Time Workflow: Keep your analyzer visible while mixing to catch problems as they develop.

What to Watch: When you add a new element, see where its energy appears. When you EQ, verify you're affecting the intended frequencies.

Processing Verification: Apply compression or saturation and watch how it affects the spectrum. Are you adding harmonics? Taming peaks?

Critical Reminder: The analyzer confirms what your ears hear. If it looks good but sounds bad, trust your ears.

Understanding FFT Size & Resolution

FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) is the algorithm that converts audio into the frequency display. The FFT size dramatically affects what you see and how responsive the analyzer is.

How FFT Size Works

  • Formula: Frequency resolution = Sample Rate √∑ FFT Size
  • Example: At 44.1 kHz with 1024 FFT: 44100 √∑ 1024 = ~43 Hz resolution
  • Higher FFT: Better frequency detail, more CPU usage, slower response
  • Lower FFT: Faster response, less detail, lower CPU usage
  • Professional Range: Most analyzers offer 512 to 16384 points

Choosing the Right FFT Size

  • 1024-2048: Fast response, good for tracking transients and drums
  • 4096: Balanced resolution and speed - most common for mixing
  • 8192-16384: High detail for mastering, finding subtle resonances
  • Low-end analysis: Larger FFT (8192+) for precise bass frequency detection
  • General mixing: 2048-4096 provides best real-time performance

Window Functions Explained

  • What They Do: Window functions reduce spectral leakage (signal bleeding into adjacent frequency bins)
  • Hann Window: Most common, good general-purpose balance
  • Blackman Window: Best for audio work, measures low-level harmonics near strong fundamentals
  • Hamming: Better frequency resolution, slightly more leakage
  • Pro Recommendation: Blackman for mixing/mastering, Hann for general use

Critical Warnings & Common Mistakes

‚ùå Don't Mix With Your Eyes

The Trap: Making EQ decisions based solely on what the spectrum looks like, not what you hear.

The Truth: There is no "correct" frequency graph. Each song sounds different, and each graph will be unique. Copying one song's spectrum to another is useless.

Right Approach: Use the analyzer to confirm what your ears hear and to catch problems you might miss, but always make the final judgment with your ears.

‚ùå Ignoring Genre Context

The Mistake: Comparing your EDM track's spectrum to an acoustic jazz reference and trying to match it.

Why It's Wrong: Different genres have completely different spectral balances. EDM emphasizes sub-bass and top-end. Acoustic jazz has strong midrange presence.

Correct Method: Only compare to references in the same genre. Study what's normal for that style, then make creative decisions within that context.

‚ùå Overcorrecting Based on Static Snapshots

The Problem: Taking a screenshot of the reference track's spectrum and trying to make yours match exactly.

Why This Fails: Music is dynamic. The spectrum constantly changes with the arrangement. A snapshot captures one moment, not the whole picture.

Professional Technique: Compare similar sections in real-time (verse to verse, chorus to chorus). Watch how the spectrum moves and breathes.
